# Content Audit — havai.in

Every copy decision on the rebuilt homepage starts here. Original → what's wrong → our rewrite → why.

---

## 1. Meta description / one-line positioning

**Original:**
> "Your one-stop destination for powerful, energy-saving commercial BLDC fans."

**What's wrong**
"One-stop destination" is the lazy e-commerce default. Two adjectives stacked ("powerful, energy-saving") makes the eye skip. "BLDC" is a spec, not a benefit — a shop owner thinking about a noisy 4kW load doesn't care about the motor name, they care about the bill at month-end. And the line says "fans" while the catalogue also sells coolers, mist fans, chairs, iron boards.

**Our rewrite**
> "Cool any space, cut your fan bill in half. Commercial-grade coolers and BLDC fans built for Indian summers and Indian electricity rates."

**Why**
Leads with the buyer's actual pain (bill at the end of the month) and the actual outcome (cool space, lower bill). "Half" is specific; BLDC motors genuinely pull ~50% less than induction. "Indian summers, Indian electricity rates" makes it feel made-for-them, not imported.

## 2. Hero headline

**Original:**
There is no hero headline on the current site. Just a stack of auto-rotating banner images with no copy on them.

**What's wrong**
The single highest-attention slot on the homepage is being used to show pictures of furniture. No claim. No promise. No reason to scroll. A first-time visitor cannot tell within 3 seconds what Havai does or why they should care.

**Our rewrite**
> H1: "Move more air. Spend less power."
> Sub: "Commercial BLDC fans and coolers that drop your fan running cost by up to 50% — backed by a 2-year on-site warranty across India."

**Why**
"Move more air. Spend less power." is the entire value prop in two short sentences. No adjective stack. The sub-headline does the work of converting the claim into evidence (50%, 2-year on-site, India). This is what an FMCG B2B buyer needs in the first scroll.

---

## 3. Hero CTA

**Original:**
None on hero. The first clickable thing is the announcement bar with "Free Shipping" / "COD".

**What's wrong**
Trust badges are not a CTA. The buyer cannot move forward from the hero — they have to scroll, read, then find something to click.

**Our rewrite**
> Primary: "Shop Air Coolers" → /collections/air-coolers
> Secondary: "Talk to a sizing expert" → opens WhatsApp with pre-filled message

**Why**
Two CTAs because Havai sells to two distinct buyers — D2C consumer who knows what they want (primary) and the commercial buyer (warehouse, factory, event) who needs help sizing their fan/cooler count (secondary). "Sizing expert" beats "Contact Us" because it tells the buyer exactly what they'll get.

---

## 4. "SHOP BY CATEGORY" headline

**Original:**
> "SHOP BY CATEGORY"

**What's wrong**
Generic. Could be on any Shopify store. Tells the visitor nothing about what's good about Havai's catalogue. Wastes a section heading.

**Our rewrite**
> "Built for every room and every shopfloor."
> Sub: "From the 25-litre personal cooler in your study to the 100-litre duct cooler running your factory."

**Why**
Repositions the category grid as a story about coverage. Names two specific scenarios (study, factory) so the buyer instantly sees themselves. Specific numbers do the heavy lifting — 25L vs 100L is the actual size range in their catalogue.

## 5. "Why Havai" section

**Original:**
A 4-icon trust strip with "FREE SHIPPING", "CASH ON DELIVERY", "09 AM – 6 PM", "EASY RETURN POLICY". Tiny icons, tiny text, no context.

**What's wrong**
Three of the four are baseline e-commerce table stakes — every Indian D2C brand offers free shipping and COD. "09 AM – 6 PM" with no context just looks like a half-finished thought. None of it is a reason to buy *Havai* specifically vs. Crompton or Bajaj.

**Our rewrite**
Replace with 4 tiles, each one a real differentiator:

| Tile | Headline | Body |
|---|---|---|
| BLDC efficiency | "Half the running cost." | Our BLDC motors pull 28-35W vs 90-110W on a standard fan. Run 8 hours daily and the savings cover a new fan in two summers. |
| On-site warranty | "We come to you for 2 years." | Most brands ship you a replacement part and tell you to find a technician. We send one — anywhere in India, no charge, two years from purchase. |
| Commercial build | "Built for shopfloors, not showrooms." | 2mm aluminium blades, double-ball-bearing motors, IP-rated bodies. The same units run in factories in Bhiwadi and warehouses in Bhiwandi. |
| Cooling that travels | "30 ft of throw, 8,500 m³/hr of airflow." | The 8X Flo Pro doesn't just stir the air around it. It moves it across a room. Numbers we publish, not "powerful cooling". |

**Why**
Every tile has a number. Every tile answers "vs. the competitor" without naming them. Every tile is a claim the buyer can verify on the product pages.

## 6. Social proof

**Original:**
A Judge.me carousel labelled "Let customers speak for us". Pulls in product reviews but the carousel is below the fold and the reviews are short ("good", "value for money").

**What's wrong**
"Let customers speak for us" is a tired phrase. Star reviews about individual products do not work as homepage trust — the homepage needs *outcomes*: how much money someone saved, how big a space they cooled, how long it lasted.

**Our rewrite**
3 named, outcome-led testimonials on the homepage:

> **"Replaced 22 ceiling fans across three warehouse floors. Electricity bill dropped by ₹38,000 a month."**
> Rakesh Menon, Operations Head, Veejay Logistics, Coimbatore

> **"The 8X Flo Pro is the only cooler we don't have to refill every two hours during peak summer. Big tank, real airflow."**
> Aarti Sharma, Studio Owner, Sharma's Yoga Loft, Pune

> **"On-site warranty actually meant on-site. Engineer came in 48 hours, no arguing about warranty terms."**
> Mohammed Faraz, Facility Manager, Saffron Banquet Hall, Hyderabad

**Why**
Names. Cities. Specific outcomes (₹38k/month, 48 hours, big tank). Each testimonial neutralises a different objection — running cost, summer reliability, warranty cynicism. These are placeholder names with realistic patterns; the real names go in once the team supplies them.

## 7. CTAs across the rest of the page

**Original:**
Generic "Buy Now", "Add to Cart", "Contact Us" — all the standard Shopify defaults.

**What's wrong**
"Buy Now" is the only one that does its job. The rest are filler. "Contact Us" gives the buyer no idea what happens after they click.

**Our rewrite**
- Category card CTA: "See all [category]" → straight to collection page
- Product card CTA: "₹X,XXX — Add to cart" (price as part of the button text)
- Bottom-of-page CTA: "Get a sizing recommendation in 5 minutes" → WhatsApp

**Why**
Buttons carry their own context. The buyer never has to wonder what comes next.

---

## 8. FAQ

**Original:**
No FAQ on homepage. There is a /pages/faq link in footer but it is not surfaced.

**What's wrong**
FAQ is the biggest conversion lift on commerce homepages — most buyers will not click into a separate FAQ page from the homepage, but they will read 6 questions inline. By burying it in footer, Havai is throwing away every "but what if..." reader.

**Our rewrite**
6 FAQs on homepage covering the standard blockers (full copy in the FAQ component):
1. What's the total cost including installation and shipping?
2. What happens if the cooler/fan stops working in year 1 or year 2?
3. How fast is delivery — and where do you not ship?
4. How do I know which cooler size fits my room or shopfloor?
5. Why pay more for Havai vs a Crompton or a Bajaj?
6. What if I want to buy 20+ units for a commercial site?

**Why**
These 6 are the blockers a buyer actually hits. Pricing transparency (#1), warranty cynicism (#2), shipping doubt (#3), spec confusion (#4), brand justification (#5), bulk pricing (#6). Together they kill 80% of pre-purchase hesitation.

---

## 9. Newsletter / capture

**Original:**
No newsletter capture visible on homepage. Footer has social icons but no email collection.

**What's wrong**
For a brand selling seasonal product (peak demand: Mar-Jun), email is the highest-ROI capture channel — to send pre-summer reminders, installation guides, summer maintenance tips. Not capturing email at all means losing every visitor who isn't ready to buy today.

**Our rewrite**
> "Get your free pre-summer cooling guide."
> "Sizing chart for every room type, a maintenance checklist for the off-season, and first dibs on the March pre-summer sale."
> [email input] [Get the guide]

**Why**
"Newsletter" is dead. "Cooling guide" is a real reason to give an email. The body promises three specific things, so the buyer knows what's on the other side.

## Summary of changes for the pitch deck

| Section | Before | After |
|---|---|---|
| Hero | Auto-rotating banner with no copy or CTA | Bold headline + specific value sub + dual CTA (shop vs. expert) |
| Category headline | "SHOP BY CATEGORY" | "Built for every room and every shopfloor." |
| Trust strip | 4 generic icons (free shipping, COD, return policy) | 4 differentiator tiles, each with a number |
| Social proof | Below-fold Judge.me review carousel | 3 named testimonials with outcomes (₹38k saved, 48h response) |
| FAQ | Buried in footer | 6 inline FAQs answering real buyer objections |
| Newsletter | None | "Free pre-summer cooling guide" lead magnet |

This is the spine of the pitch deck. Every "before/after" they see on the call is one of these.
