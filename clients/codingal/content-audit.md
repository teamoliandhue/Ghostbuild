# Content Audit — codingal.com

Every line of homepage copy starts here. Original → what's wrong → our rewrite → why. Voice: senior human copywriter. Short, specific, parent-facing. No em dashes.

---

## 1. Hero headline

**Original:**
> "Top-rated online programming, Coding & AI classes for kids to become the innovators of tomorrow"

**What's wrong**
It is a slogan, not a promise. "Innovators of tomorrow" is the exact phrase on every edtech site. It stacks three category words (programming, coding, AI) and never tells a parent what their child will actually get or when.

**Our rewrite**
> H1: "Your child writes their first real program in week one."
> Sub: "Live 1-on-1 coding and AI classes for ages 5 to 17, taught by vetted CS instructors. Trusted by a million students across 135 countries."

**Why**
Outcome-first and time-bound. "Week one" makes the promise concrete and beatable. The sub does the proof work (live, 1-on-1, ages, vetted, the million-student number) in one breath. Carried over from the redesign mockup the team approved.

---

## 2. Hero CTA

**Original:**
Mixed labels across the page: "Try a free lesson", "Sign Up", "Join class", "Try free".

**What's wrong**
Four labels for one action. Every inconsistent CTA is a small moment of doubt about what happens next.

**Our rewrite**
> Primary (everywhere): "Book a free trial class"
> Secondary: "See the courses"

**Why**
One primary verb across the whole page. "Book a free trial class" tells the parent exactly what they get and that it costs nothing. Course cards use the same family ("Try free") tightened to match.

---

## 3. Trust placement

**Original:**
Heavy stats + investor logos + press appear immediately after the hero, before any product explanation.

**What's wrong**
The parent trusts Codingal before they understand it. Proof with nothing to attach to.

**Our rewrite**
Thin credibility strip under the hero (one rating line + "Backed by Y Combinator, Google, Amazon"). Full trust section moves below the product story.

**Why**
Slim proof high, deep proof low. Resolves the contradiction flagged in audit.md (UX-1).

---

## 4. How it works

**Original:**
A passive 3-card strip with no sense of progression.

**Our rewrite**
> Section: "From booking to a finished project in three steps."
> 1. Book a free trial class — Pick a time that suits you. Your child meets a real instructor 1-on-1 and builds something live in the first session. No payment to try.
> 2. We match the right course — After the trial we recommend a course mapped to your child's grade and pace, from the first block of code through to AP Computer Science.
> 3. Watch real projects ship — Your child builds games, apps and websites every few weeks. You track every project and report from the parent dashboard.

**Why**
Each step ends in something the parent can picture. Connected timeline, not three loose cards.

---

## 5. Courses

**Original:**
> "Choose an AI & coding course that excites your child"
Cards show grade range, title, duration only.

**What's wrong**
Generic heading. Cards miss skill level, age fit, and the outcome, so a parent can't self-select.

**Our rewrite**
> Heading: "15 courses, mapped to your child's grade. From first block of code to AP Computer Science."
> Sub: "Every course is a structured path with real projects at the end of each stage. Here are nine of the most popular."
> Each card: grade badge + level (Beginner/Intermediate/Advanced) + title + one-line outcome + lessons/duration + "Try free".

Sample card outcomes:
- Scratch Programming with AI (Grade 1-8, Beginner): "First taste of code. Kids build animations and games by dragging blocks."
- Roblox Champion (Grade 4-8, Intermediate): "Build and design epic Roblox worlds other kids actually play."
- Python Champion (Grade 6-12, Intermediate): "Move from blocks to real Python, the language pros use daily."
- Website Development (Grade 8-12, Advanced): "Build and launch real AI-powered websites from scratch."

**Why**
Level + age + outcome on every card lets a parent pick in seconds (audit UX-4). Real course names and art carried from the live catalogue.

---

## 6. What a class looks like (NEW)

**Original:** Nothing. The site never shows the live class.

**Our rewrite**
> "See exactly what a class looks like."
> "One instructor, one child, one screen. Your child codes live while the teacher guides, never a recording, never a crowded room. Here's the dashboard you both get afterwards."
> + dashboard screenshot.

**Why**
The single biggest unanswered question for live 1-on-1 instruction (audit UX-2). Uses the real dashboard image.

## 7. Student projects

**Original:**
> "See amazing projects created by our students"
Below the fold, weak.

**Our rewrite**
> "Real projects, built by real kids."
> "Every one of these shipped at the end of a course stage. Grade shown on each."
> Real projects: Premier league voting website (Grade 10), Dance party animation (Grade 3), Solar system (Grade 2), Rocket launch in Python (Grade 3), Life cycle of a butterfly (Grade 7), Knowing about animals in Scratch (Grade 6).

**Why**
Strongest outcome proof for a parent. Real student work with grade tags (audit UI-2). Restored after the mockup dropped it.

---

## 8. Teachers

**Original:**
> "Learn to code from experienced teachers"
Text-heavy.

**Our rewrite**
> "Taught by the top 1% of instructors we interview. Every one a CS graduate. Every one background-checked."
> "The right teacher is the whole game in 1-on-1 learning. We hire hard so you never have to wonder who is on the other side of the screen."
> Checklist: every instructor a CS graduate from the top 1% of applicants · background checks before any instructor meets a child · 1-on-1 so the class moves at your child's pace · parent-teacher meetings after the 6th and 12th session with written reports.
> Stat chips: 1000+ CS-graduate instructors · 7+ yrs average coding experience · 91% women educators · top 1% of applicants hired.

**Why**
Child safety is a parent's first fear. Background checks and "who is on the other side of the screen" hit it directly.

---

## 9. Why parents trust Codingal (full trust)

**Original:**
Stats scattered, uncontextualised: "4.6/5", "1,000,000+", "Top 1%".

**What's wrong**
Numbers with no source read as marketing.

**Our rewrite**
> "Why a million parents chose Codingal."
> 1,000,000+ students signed up · 4.6/5 from 20,000+ parent reviews · 135 countries · backed by Y Combinator, Google and Amazon · as seen in Mint, Indian Express, YourStory, Business Standard, The Print.

**Why**
Every number gets its source (audit UX-6). "4.6/5 from 20,000+ parent reviews" beats a bare "4.6/5".

---

## 10. Pricing (NEW on page)

**Original:** Fully gated. No price anywhere.

**Our rewrite**
> "Simple plans. Free trial first, full refund if it's not a fit."
> Three plan cards (directional, placeholder amounts for team to confirm):
> - Explorer — 1 class / week. Best for first-timers. From $X / month.
> - Builder — 2 classes / week. Most popular. From $Y / month.
> - Pro — 3+ classes / week. Fastest progress. From $Z / month.
> Line under: "Every plan starts with a free trial class. 30-day money-back guarantee, no questions."

**Why**
Hiding price is the biggest blocker (audit UX-3). Directional pricing + the existing money-back guarantee removes it. Amounts flagged for team confirmation, never invented as final.

---

## 11. FAQ (real parent objections)

**Original:** Generic FAQ, buried.

**Our rewrite** (6 inline):
1. How much does it cost, and what's included?
2. Is my child too young, or too old, to start?
3. What if my child loses interest or we want to stop?
4. How is this better than free coding videos on YouTube?
5. How much screen time does this actually add?
6. What do we need, a special computer or software?

**Why**
The blockers a parent actually hits: price, age fit, commitment risk, "why pay", screen-time guilt, setup friction (audit UX-5).

---

## 12. Final CTA + Footer

**Our rewrite**
> "Book your child's free trial class."
> "One live session, one real project, zero payment. See if it clicks before you decide anything."
> Primary: "Book a free trial class".

Footer: courses by grade, learning paths, company, resources, policies, social. Penguin (Cody) kept as a small brand accent in logo + footer.
